Do you remember who you were when you were fourteen years old? You probably don’t. Even if you remember those embarrassing moments, the pubescent pains of the world and your attempts to deal with them through cranked-up MCR and a terrible fringe haircut, those are not you. At least, not anymore.

You are, now, who you are. Your viewport in the rolodex of memory is filtered through your mind as it exists. Your biases, your loves, hates, regrets, and politics—all the things you are—color this process of remembrance. Perhaps this is why you so emphatically insist on writing things down as they happen. A diary, though the words will be interpreted through your eyes and mind just as anything else, aliases less than your memory. It is a more trustworthy archival medium.

A more salient container for your humanity, you might find, lies in your old social accounts. If your parents were moderately irresponsible, they might have provided you with access to something like a smartphone, and if they were especially so, you may have had something like Instagram, Tumblr, or the like. Have you ever looked back at them? The flurry of strange, immature posts?

Perhaps these express our humanity in all its rough edges much better than any diary. Those vague, Mitski-playing stories about your freshman-year breakup; those memes that you question ever being funny in the slightest, the 'rawr ecks-dee’ of it all. Even if it's unrecognizable to you now, perhaps some latent trace of your soul exists there, on the network, frozen in time and unchanging. When did you last greet it?
